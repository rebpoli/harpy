especially inspired by \parencite{cheng2016} and \parencite{nield2006} that look cleaner and more intuitive.

\subsection{Mechanics}
The mechanical equilibrium states that:
\begin{align}
  \nabla \cdot \ssigma + \bb = 0
\end{align}
where $\bb$ is the body force, typically associated to the gravity acceleration.
The Cauchy total stress $\ssigma$ is defined as:
\begin{align}
    \ssigma = \CC : \eeps - \alpha\ P\ \II - \alpha_d\ \II\ T
\label{eqn:sigtot_def}
\end{align}
where $\CC$ is the elastic stiffness tensor,
\[C_{ijkl} = \lambda \delta_{ij} \delta_{kl} + \mu ( \delta_{ik} \delta_{jl} 
            + \delta_{il} \delta_{kj} ) \qquad,\] 
the Lame parameters are 
\[\lambda=\frac{3K\nu}{1+\nu} \qquad ,\]
and
\[\mu=\frac{3K(1-2\nu)}{2(1+\nu)}  \qquad ,\]
$\nu$ is the Poisson coefficient,
$\eeps=\frac{1}{2} ( \nabla \uu + \nabla^T \uu )$ is the strain tensor, 
$\uu$ is the displacement vector, $\alpha$ is the Biot coefficient, $\alpha_d=K\beta_d$ is the 
drained thermoelastic effective stress coefficient, $K$ is the drained bulk moduluis,
$\beta_d=3\beta_l$ is the drained coefficient
of volumetric thermal expansion, $\beta_l$ is the coefficient of linear thermal expansion, $P$ is
the pore pressure and $T$ is the temperature.

With the above in mind, equation \eqref{eqn:sigtot_def} can be written as \parencite{coussy2004}
\[\ssigma = (K - \frac{2}{3}\ \mu)\ \text{tr}(\eeps)\  \II + 2\ \mu\ \eeps \qquad,\]
or in index notation
\[\sigma_{ij}=(K - \frac{2}{3}\ \mu)\ \varepsilon_{kk}\ \delta_{ij} + 2\ \mu\ \varepsilon_{ij} \qquad.\]

\subsection{Heat transport - thermal}
We refer to work by \parencite{liu2009} and \parencite{coussy2004}.
Most o the notation we borrow from \parencite{cheng2016}.
\begin{align}
  0           &=  \alpha_d\ \Tzero\ \dot{\epsilon} - \beta_e\ \Tzero\ \dot{P} 
                  + \rhocm\ \dot{T} + \nabla\cdot\qq_T  \\
  \qq_T       &= - \kappam\ \nabla T + \rhocf\ T\ \nabla P \\
  (\rho c)_m  &= (1-\phi) \rhocs + \phi \rhocf \qquad \\
  \kappam     &= (1-\phi) \kappas + \phi \kappaf \qquad
\end{align}
where $\epsilon=\varepsilon_{kk}$ is the volumetric strain, $\beta_e$ is the coefficient of 
volumetric thermal expansion for variation in fluid
content at constant frame volume, $\Tzero$ is the initial temperature, assuming 
\[|T-\Tzero|/\Tzero\ll1 \qquad ,\]
$\rho$ is the density of the fluid $f$, solid $s$
or the mixture $m$, and $c$ is the specific heat of each phase, $\kappa$ is the thermal conductivity
of each phase, $\phi$ is the porosity, $\qq_T$ is the heat flux given by Fourier's Law.

%
%
%
\subsection{Hydraulic flow}
\begin{align}
  0 &= \alpha\ \dot{\epsilon} + \frac{1}{M}\ \dot{P} - \beta_e\ \dot{T} + \nabla\cdot \qq_P \\
  \qq_P &= - k\ \nabla P 
\end{align}
where $M$ is the Biot modulus defined by
\[ \frac{1}{M} = {\frac{\phi}{K_f} + \frac{\alpha - \phi}{K_s} \qquad,\]
or equivalentely,
\[ \frac{1}{M} = \frac{\phi}{K_f} + \frac{1-\phi}{K_s} - \frac{K}{K_s^2}\]
as
\[\alpha=1- \frac{K}{K_s} \qquad.\]
For Darcy's law,
\[k=\hat{k}/\mu\] 
is the 
hydraulic conductivity, $\hat{k}$ is the intrinsic permeability and $\mu$ is the fluid 
viscosity (assuming single phase), $\qq_P$ is the fluid flux given by Darcy's law.
